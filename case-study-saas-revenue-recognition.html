Case study breakdown

How the workflow changed from manual effort to controlled automation

Pages here follow the Ledger Summit story structure: context first, implementation detail second, measured results third, and controls throughout.

Before automation

Revenue recognition lived in two spreadsheets: one for committed ARR and one for usage. Contract modifications required manual recalculation of cumulative catch-up. The auditor flagged 7 modifications the prior year that had been computed inconsistently.

  • Manual ASC 606 revenue schedule per contract, in spreadsheets
  • Usage-based revenue posted from billing system separately
  • Contract modifications recalculated by hand
  • Deferred revenue waterfall built from spreadsheets at quarter-end
  • Auditor findings on modification cumulative-catch-up calculations

What Ledger Summit implemented

We built a contract-aware revenue engine that pulls from the CRM and billing system, applies ASC 606 logic, generates revenue recognition JEs, and produces a per-contract evidence pack. Contract modifications run through documented logic with reviewer sign-off.

  • Contract-level rev-rec engine integrated with Salesforce and Stripe
  • ASC 606 logic: performance obligations, transaction price, allocation, recognition
  • Contract modification handling: prospective vs. cumulative catch-up, with reviewer sign-off
  • Usage-based revenue tied to source data with reconciliation
  • Deferred revenue waterfall auto-generated each close
  • Per-contract evidence pack: contract data, allocations, JE trace, reviewer log

Implementation timeline

  • Week 1–2: Contract inventory, performance obligation analysis, policy memo refresh
  • Week 3–5: Built rev-rec engine, integrations, modification logic
  • Week 6: Parallel run for one full month
  • Week 7: Full cutover; first close on the new system
  • Week 8–10: Hypercare, rule refinement, auditor walk-through prep

Controls and audit-ready evidence

  • Per-contract trace from contract data → allocation → JE → revenue schedule
  • Modification logic documented with worked examples; reviewer sign-off required
  • Usage data reconciled to source billing system each close
  • Deferred revenue waterfall ties to GL roll-forward
  • ASC 606 policy memo refreshed; auditor walk-through script prepared

Notable policy decisions

  • Termination-for-convenience: treated as a single performance obligation period; documented and accepted by auditor
  • Material rights (renewal options): assessed; concluded immaterial; documented
  • Standalone selling price: re-estimated annually; documented methodology
  • Variable consideration (usage): expected-value method; constrained where not estimable

Frequently asked questions

Did you migrate to a rev-rec product like RightRev or Zenskar?

No. We built the engine on top of the existing NetSuite + Salesforce + Stripe stack. The client evaluated RightRev and Zenskar; for this scale ($50M ARR, vertical SaaS) the build-and-integrate path delivered faster and cost less. Above ~$200M ARR or with very complex commerce, a dedicated product often makes sense.

How did the auditor react?

Positive. The evidence pack walked them through every contract with allocations, JEs, and reviewer sign-off. They asked for 4 sample contracts, all passed without follow-up requests. Audit fieldwork dropped 1.5 days for the rev-rec area.

How do you handle complex contract modifications?

Modification logic is documented (prospective vs. cumulative catch-up, blended pricing, separate contracts) with worked examples in the controls package. The system flags modifications that require judgment; a reviewer signs off before posting.
