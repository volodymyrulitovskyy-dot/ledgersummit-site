Guide Below The Tool

How to use the Roth vs Traditional IRA calculator well

Key concepts, practical steps, and decision guidance for retirement account selection.

What it is

A Roth vs Traditional IRA calculator compares the after-tax retirement value of each account type by applying different tax treatment to contributions and withdrawals over your accumulation period.

Who it is for

Anyone deciding between Roth and Traditional IRA contributions, financial advisors modeling client scenarios, and retirement planners evaluating tax diversification strategies.

What matters most

The relationship between your current marginal tax rate and your expected retirement tax rate is the single biggest driver. Time horizon and contribution amount amplify the difference.

Workflow

Four practical steps

Use this calculator during open enrollment, when changing jobs, or during annual financial planning reviews.

1
Enter your annual contribution amount.

Use the maximum allowed ($7,000 in 2024, $8,000 if 50+) or your planned contribution.

2
Set your current and retirement tax rates.

Your current marginal rate is on your tax return. Estimate retirement rate based on expected income sources.

3
Enter ages and expected return.

The gap between current age and retirement age determines the compounding period. Use 6-8% for a diversified portfolio.

4
Compare the after-tax outcomes.

The calculator shows which account type produces more after-tax wealth. Use this to guide your contribution decision.

Review Checklist

What to validate first

Key assumptions that affect the Roth vs Traditional comparison most.

Current marginal tax rate accuracy

Your marginal rate (not effective rate) determines the tax cost of Roth contributions and the tax savings of Traditional contributions.

Retirement tax rate estimate

Consider Social Security income, pension, RMDs, and other retirement income when estimating your future tax bracket.

Investment return assumption

Higher returns amplify the difference between account types. Use conservative estimates for planning.

RMD impact on Traditional IRA

Required minimum distributions starting at age 73 force taxable withdrawals that can push you into higher brackets.

Income limits for Roth contributions

Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at higher incomes ($161,000-$176,000 for single filers in 2024).

Tax diversification value

Having both Roth and Traditional balances gives flexibility to manage taxable income in retirement.
