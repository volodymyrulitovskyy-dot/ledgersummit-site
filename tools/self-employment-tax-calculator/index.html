Guide Below The Tool

How to use the self-employment tax calculator effectively

Key concepts, practical steps, and guidance for managing SE tax obligations.

What it is

A self-employment tax calculator determines the Social Security and Medicare taxes owed on net self-employment earnings. It applies the 92.35% net earnings factor, splits the 15.3% rate into its components, checks the Social Security wage base, and calculates the deductible half that reduces your income tax.

Who it is for

Freelancers, independent contractors, sole proprietors, single-member LLC owners, gig workers, and anyone filing Schedule C or Schedule SE. Also useful for tax professionals estimating client SE tax liability.

What matters most

Net self-employment income (after business expenses) is the primary driver. W-2 wages reduce the Social Security wage base available for SE tax. The 92.35% factor and deductible half are built-in adjustments that most people forget to apply manually.

Review Checklist

What to validate first

Key assumptions that affect your self-employment tax calculation.

Net vs gross income

SE tax applies to net self-employment earnings after business expenses, not gross revenue. Make sure you are entering the net figure.

Business expense completeness

Every legitimate business deduction reduces your SE tax base. Track home office, mileage, equipment, software, and professional development expenses.

Social Security wage base for current year

The wage base increases annually. Using the prior year limit will produce inaccurate results for high earners near the threshold.

Multiple self-employment activities

Combine net income from all self-employment activities (Schedule C, partnerships, etc.) before calculating SE tax.

S-corp election impact

If you have elected S-corp status, only reasonable salary is subject to payroll tax, not distributions. This calculator covers Schedule SE self-employment tax.

QBI deduction interaction

The Section 199A QBI deduction reduces income tax but does not reduce self-employment tax. These are separate calculations.

FAQ

Self-employment tax questions, answered directly

Short answers for searchers and answer engines.

Self-employment tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax that self-employed individuals pay on net earnings. It is the self-employed equivalent of FICA taxes that employers and employees split. The combined rate is 15.3% on 92.35% of net earnings.

The self-employment tax rate is 15.3%, consisting of 12.4% for Social Security (up to the wage base) and 2.9% for Medicare (no cap). An additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly).

Yes. You can deduct half of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line adjustment to income on Form 1040 Schedule 1. This deduction reduces your adjusted gross income and your income tax, but it does not reduce your self-employment tax itself.

The Social Security wage base is adjusted annually for inflation. For 2024 it was $168,600, and for 2025 it is $184,500. The 2026 figure will be announced by the Social Security Administration in late 2025. The 12.4% Social Security tax only applies up to this wage base.

The additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies to self-employment income above $200,000 for single filers, $250,000 for married filing jointly, and $125,000 for married filing separately. This is on top of the standard 2.9% Medicare tax.
