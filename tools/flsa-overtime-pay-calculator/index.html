Review Checklist

What to validate first

Key details that affect FLSA overtime pay accuracy.

Regular rate accuracy

The regular rate must include all remuneration except specifically excluded payments. Shift differentials and non-discretionary bonuses are always included.

Shift differential inclusion

Shift premiums paid for working particular shifts must be added to base pay before dividing by total hours to get the regular rate.

Bonus inclusion

Non-discretionary bonuses (production, attendance, safety, quality) must be included. Discretionary bonuses (holiday gifts, year-end bonuses at employer discretion) are excluded.

OT multiplier

Federal FLSA requires 1.5x for hours over 40. Some states require daily overtime or double-time. Use the correct multiplier for your jurisdiction.

Total hours

Count all hours actually worked. Training time, on-call time (if restricted), and travel time may be compensable hours under FLSA.

DOL compliance

Regular rate errors are among the top DOL violation categories. The penalty includes back pay, liquidated damages, and potential civil penalties per violation.

FAQ

FLSA Overtime Calculator for Payroll Teams questions, answered directly

Short answers for searchers and answer engines.

The FLSA regular rate is the weighted average hourly rate including base wage, shift differentials, and non-discretionary bonuses. It is calculated by dividing total straight-time compensation by total hours worked.

Calculate the regular rate by dividing total straight-time earnings by total hours. Multiply the regular rate by 0.5 times overtime hours for the premium. Add the premium to straight-time pay for total gross wages.

The regular rate must include base wages, shift differentials, non-discretionary bonuses, piece rate earnings, and commissions. It excludes discretionary bonuses, gifts, vacation pay, and employer benefit contributions.

The regular rate is higher because it includes shift differentials and non-discretionary bonuses on top of the base wage. Calculating overtime on only the base rate understates the premium and violates FLSA.

Penalties include back pay for underpaid overtime, liquidated damages equal to the back pay (doubling liability), civil penalties up to $2,451 per violation, and attorney fees. Regular rate miscalculation is one of the most common DOL findings.
