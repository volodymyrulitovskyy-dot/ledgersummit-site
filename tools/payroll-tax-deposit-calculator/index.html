Guide Below The Tool

How to use the payroll tax deposit calculator well

Key concepts, practical steps, and guidance for staying compliant with IRS deposit rules.

What it is

A payroll tax deposit calculator determines your federal payroll tax deposit schedule based on the IRS lookback period rule and calculates the total deposit amount by summing federal income tax withholding, employee Social Security and Medicare, and employer Social Security and Medicare for each pay period.

Who it is for

Small business owners running payroll, bookkeepers and payroll administrators responsible for tax deposits, accountants verifying deposit calculations, and anyone transitioning from a payroll service who needs to understand the deposit mechanics.

What matters most

Deposit timing is non-negotiable. The IRS imposes escalating penalties for late deposits starting at 2% and climbing to 15%. Your prior-year liability determines your schedule, and the $100,000 next-day rule can change your obligations mid-year.

Review Checklist

What to validate first

Key assumptions and details that affect your payroll tax deposit accuracy.

Lookback period accuracy

The lookback period is not the calendar year. It runs July 1 through June 30, two years before the current year. Verify you are using the correct period.

Social Security wage base limit

Social Security tax (6.2%) only applies to wages up to the annual wage base ($184,500 for 2026). Verify that high earners are not over-withheld.

Additional Medicare tax for high earners

Employees earning over $200,000 owe an additional 0.9% Medicare tax. This is employee-only and does not affect the employer share.

State payroll taxes are separate

This calculator covers federal payroll tax deposits only. State withholding, SUTA, and other state taxes have separate deposit schedules.

De minimis deposit exception

If your total tax liability for the quarter is less than $2,500, you may pay with your Form 941 instead of making deposits. Verify whether you qualify.

Penalty rates escalate quickly

Deposits 1-5 days late incur a 2% penalty. 6-15 days late is 5%. Over 15 days is 10%. More than 10 days after an IRS notice is 15%. Timeliness matters.

FAQ

Payroll tax deposit questions, answered directly

Short answers for searchers and answer engines.

Your deposit schedule is based on your lookback period tax liability. If you reported $50,000 or less during the lookback period (July 1 through June 30, two years prior), you are a monthly depositor. Above $50,000, you are semi-weekly. New employers default to monthly.

Semi-weekly depositors deposit by Wednesday for paydays on Wednesday, Thursday, or Friday, and by Friday for paydays on Saturday, Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday. You have three banking days after the semi-weekly period ends.

You must deposit federal income tax withheld, the employee share of Social Security and Medicare, and the employer share of Social Security and Medicare. These are reported on Form 941 and deposited via EFTPS.

If you accumulate $100,000 or more in tax liability on any single day, you must deposit by the next business day. This applies regardless of your normal schedule and switches monthly depositors to semi-weekly for the rest of the year.

The IRS imposes penalties from 2% (1-5 days late) up to 15% (more than 10 days after an IRS notice). Interest also accrues on the unpaid amount. Using EFTPS with calendar reminders helps avoid these costly penalties.
